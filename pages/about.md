## About This Project

In her contribution to Black Queer Studies: A Critical Anthology (also the first critical anthology on Black Queer Studies), Black lesbian feminist writer Jewelle Gomez closes her critical essay, “But Some of Us Are Brave Lesbians: The Absence of Black Lesbian Fiction,” with the evergreen reminder: “A syllabus is a terrible thing to waste.” Through this project, I set out to constellate black lesbian writers across page and time as a pedagogical resource for teachers who seek to enliven their courses with the words, lives, dreams, and worlds black lesbian writers offer within and across anthologies. 

As it presently stands, this project brings together data from eight anthologies spanning from 1970 to 2021, however, I plan to continue adding data from more anthologies as I come to meet them. If there is an anthology missing, or a misrepresentation from an anthology you notice in your exploration of this site/project, please let me know (amg379@cornell.edu). 
 
While only some of the anthologies I’ve reviewed have been republished by mainstream or academic presses and are thus more widely available, those older or out-of-print anthologies remain available for purchase through used and thrifted bookstore outlets (on and offline). I highly recommend seeking out an anthology of your own from your favorite local bookstore! 

Note on Metadata Schemes and Methodology:

For this project, “black lesbian” represents not only an identity shared among many of the authors, but also a way of being/becoming and a field of thought (“Black Lesbian Feminist Thought”) that emerges through field-defining anthologies like Home Girls (19xx) and Mouths of Rain (2021). With this in mind, rather than determine who falls into the category of “lesbian,” I relied primarily on the subject of the anthologies and contributor biographies to pull out black lesbian writers from each anthology. For example, in anthologies like Piece Of My Heart: A Lesbian of Colour Anthology (1991) and Afrekete: An Anthology of Black Lesbian Writing (1995), I consider each writer in these lesbian-centered anthologies as writing within a community of (black) lesbian writers, where “lesbian” denotes a mode of relation and thematic convention shared amongst writers. However, in anthologies like This Bridge Called My Back: Writings By Radical Women of Color (1981) and Home Girls: A Black Feminist Anthology (1983), I researched each black writer and pull out those who more explicitly identify themselves as lesbians. For those authors who do not identify as lesbians themselves, but who are published within lesbian-centered anthologies (for example, Alice Walker), I chose to include them in the data for lesbian-centered anthologies while excluding them from the data for other anthology projects. I remain interested in the ways that these shifting registers of “black lesbian” across anthologies (as identity, praxis, field of thought, mode of relation, and more) operates generatively such that black queer writers, regardless of the lesbian or non-lesbian specifics of their identity, have been able to home their literature within many of these lesbian-feminist anthological projects.
